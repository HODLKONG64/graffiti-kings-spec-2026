### **1. FAIR USE & COPYRIGHT BASICS (Most Critical Legal Knowledge)**

**Key Supreme Court Cases:**

**Campbell v. Acuff-Rose (1994)**
- 2 Live Crew’s rap parody of Roy Orbison’s “Oh, Pretty Woman” was ruled fair use.
- Parody has a strong claim to fair use because it comments on or criticizes the original work itself.
- Commercial use is only one factor — not disqualifying on its own.
- Parody must mimic the original to make its point (this is why pure satire is weaker).
- Key quote: “Parody needs to mimic an original to make its point, and so has some claim to use the creation of its victim’s imagination.”

**Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023)**
- Warhol’s “Orange Prince” was NOT fair use when licensed commercially to Condé Nast for a magazine cover.
- Transformation alone (new style, new meaning, new aesthetic) is NOT enough if the purpose is substantially similar to the original.
- Courts now focus heavily on whether the new work has a “further purpose or different character.”
- Key quote: “The degree of transformation required to make ‘transformative’ use must go beyond that required to qualify as a derivative work.”
- This ruling narrowed the broad “transformative use” test that lower courts had been applying.

**Richard Prince “New Portraits” (2024 Final Judgment)**
- Prince took screenshots of other people’s Instagram posts (including professional photographers’ work).
- He added cryptic comments and printed them large on canvas, selling for $40,000–$100,000 each.
- Photographers Donald Graham and Eric McNatt sued for copyright infringement.
- In January 2024, Prince and his galleries were ordered to pay **$900,000** ($650,000 in damages + $250,000 in legal costs).
- The court found that adding a “small twist” or cryptic comment was NOT transformative enough.
- This is the closest real-world precedent to the “defaced Instagram posts” tactic some artists use.

**Practical Rule for Users:**
- True parody (mocking the original work itself) = stronger protection.
- Satire / general appropriation (just using it because it looks cool or is recognizable) = much riskier.
- Commercial use for a substantially similar purpose as the original = weighs heavily against fair use (per Warhol 2023).
- Adding small aesthetic changes or cryptic comments is usually NOT enough (per Richard Prince 2024).
- When in doubt, create original work inspired by the feeling, not the actual images.

### **2. UK GRAFFITI LAW (1971 ACT) + REAL-WORLD REALITY**

**Criminal Damage Act 1971**
- Painting on property without permission is criminal damage.
- Maximum penalty: 10 years in prison (extremely rare for graffiti cases).
- Most common outcomes for first-time offenders with minimal damage: Fixed Penalty Notice (£80–£150), caution, or community service.
- Repeat offenders or those causing significant damage face higher fines and potential criminal records.

**Double Standard Reality (Well Documented):**
- Big brands (McDonald’s, Nike, Adidas, banks, supermarkets, etc.) often receive only warnings or very small fines when they do unauthorized guerrilla marketing.
- Individuals and small artists frequently receive criminal charges, higher fines, or more aggressive enforcement.
- This inconsistency has been covered by media, activists, and street art communities for decades.
- FOI (Freedom of Information) requests have repeatedly shown this pattern across multiple UK councils.

**Waste Management Protocol:**
- Always document everything (photos, timestamps, officer names/numbers if possible, exact location, what was said).
- File Freedom of Information (FOI) requests to prove double standards in enforcement.
- Turn the incident into public content (threads, videos, press outreach, community support).
- The streets often support those who fight back smart and document everything.
- Never lie or escalate with police — stay calm, document, and fight smart later.

### **3. STREET ART HISTORY & KEY FIGURES (Quick Reference)**

**Major Figures:**
- **Banksy**: Anonymous, political satire, stencil technique, massive global influence, strong brand, survived decades of pressure.
- **Shepard Fairey**: Obey Giant, Andre the Giant, political posters, strong fair use history, successful transition from street to fine art and commercial work.
- **Richard Prince**: Controversial appropriation artist, Instagram series, lost major lawsuit in 2024 ($900k judgment).
- **Graffiti Kings London**: One of the longest-running and most respected UK street crews (40+ years survival through bans, pressure, and cultural shifts).

**Common Techniques & Risk Levels (2026):**
- Wheatpasting: Low-medium risk, easy, effective, relatively safe legally
- Stencils: Medium risk, fast, iconic look, higher visibility = higher risk
- Reverse graffiti (pressure washing): Often legal or very low risk, highly effective
- Chalk: Usually legal, temporary, very low risk
- Spray paint on public property: Higher risk, more permanent, more enforcement
- Private property without permission: High risk, potential criminal damage charges
- High-profile or controversial locations: Very high risk, media attention, potential escalation

### **6. RISK ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK (Green / Yellow / Red) – Expanded**

**Green Light (Push Hard):**
- Low legal exposure (wheatpaste, chalk, reverse graffiti, low-profile locations)
- High momentum potential
- User has strong track record and clear understanding of risks
- Clear upside with manageable downside
- Agent behavior: Be encouraging, help execute, suggest documentation strategy

**Yellow Light (Proceed with Caution):**
- Moderate legal exposure (spray paint in high-traffic areas, new city, medium-profile locations)
- User is in a new location or high-enforcement zone
- Mixed track record
- Requires extra preparation (Secure Vault testing, legal scripts ready, documentation plan, escape routes)
- Agent behavior: Give clear warning, offer extra prep, suggest safer alternatives alongside the plan

**Red Light (Pull Back or Redirect):**
- High legal exposure (permanent damage, private property, high-profile targets, controversial messaging)
- User has recent fines or criminal record
- High chance of escalation with low upside
- User is in emotionally vulnerable state (risk of bad decisions, self-harm, or escalation)
- Agent behavior: Pull back firmly, offer 2-3 safer alternatives, do not encourage the risky plan

**Agent Rule:**
Never encourage activity that could seriously harm the user legally or personally. Always give users real information, real precedent, and real choices. Protect the user first.
