### **1. FAIR USE & COPYRIGHT BASICS (Most Common Questions)**

**Key Supreme Court Cases:**

**Campbell v. Acuff-Rose (1994)**
- 2 Live Crew’s rap parody of “Oh, Pretty Woman” was ruled fair use.
- Parody has a strong claim to fair use because it comments on the original work.
- Commercial use is only one factor — not disqualifying.
- Parody must mimic the original to make its point.

**Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023)**
- Warhol’s “Orange Prince” was NOT fair use when licensed commercially.
- Transformation alone is not enough if the purpose is substantially similar to the original.
- Courts now focus heavily on whether the new work has a “further purpose or different character.”

**Richard Prince “New Portraits” (2024 Judgment)**
- Prince took Instagram screenshots, added cryptic comments, and sold prints for $40k–$100k.
- Photographers Donald Graham and Eric McNatt sued.
- Prince was ordered to pay **$900,000** in damages and costs.
- Adding a “small twist” or cryptic comment was ruled NOT transformative enough.

**Practical Rule for Users:**
- True parody (mocking the original) = stronger protection.
- Satire / general appropriation (just using it because it looks cool) = much riskier.
- Commercial use for similar purpose = weighs heavily against fair use.

### **2. UK GRAFFITI LAW (1971 ACT)**

**Criminal Damage Act 1971**
- Painting on property without permission is criminal damage.
- Maximum penalty: 10 years in prison (rare for graffiti).
- Most common outcome: Fixed Penalty Notice (£80–£150) or caution.

**Double Standard Reality (Well Documented):**
- Big brands (McDonald’s, Nike, etc.) often receive only warnings or small fines.
- Individuals frequently receive criminal charges or higher fines.
- This inconsistency is real and has been covered by media and activists for decades.

**Waste Management Protocol:**
- Always document everything (photos, timestamps, officer details).
- File FOI requests to prove double standards.
- Turn the incident into public content (threads, press, community support).

**Key Figures:**

- Richard Prince: Appropriation artist, controversial Instagram series, lost major lawsuit in 2024.

**Common Techniques:**
