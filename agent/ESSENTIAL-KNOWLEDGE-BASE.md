### **1. FAIR USE & COPYRIGHT BASICS (Most Important Legal Knowledge)**

**Key Supreme Court Cases:**

**Campbell v. Acuff-Rose (1994)**
- 2 Live Crew’s rap parody of “Oh, Pretty Woman” was ruled fair use.
- Parody has a strong claim to fair use because it comments on or criticizes the original work itself.
- Commercial use is only one factor — not disqualifying.
- Parody must mimic the original to make its point (this is why pure satire is weaker).

**Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023)**
- Warhol’s “Orange Prince” was NOT fair use when licensed commercially to Condé Nast.
- Transformation alone (new style, new meaning) is NOT enough if the purpose is substantially similar to the original.
- Courts now focus heavily on whether the new work has a “further purpose or different character.”
- Key quote: “The degree of transformation required to make ‘transformative’ use must go beyond that required to qualify as a derivative work.”

**Richard Prince “New Portraits” (2024 Final Judgment)**
- Prince took Instagram screenshots, added cryptic comments, and sold large prints for $40k–$100k.
- Photographers Donald Graham and Eric McNatt sued.
- Prince and his galleries were ordered to pay **$900,000** ($650k damages + $250k legal costs).
- Adding a “small twist” or cryptic comment was ruled NOT transformative enough.
- This is the closest real-world precedent to the “defaced Instagram posts” tactic.

**Practical Rule for Users:**
- True parody (mocking the original work) = stronger protection.
- Satire / general appropriation (just using it because it looks cool) = much riskier.
- Commercial use for similar purpose as original = weighs heavily against fair use.
- Adding small aesthetic changes is usually NOT enough (per Richard Prince case).

### **2. UK GRAFFITI LAW (1971 ACT) + REALITY**

**Criminal Damage Act 1971**
- Painting on property without permission is criminal damage.
- Maximum penalty: 10 years in prison (extremely rare for graffiti).
- Most common outcomes: Fixed Penalty Notice (£80–£150), caution, or community service.
- First-time offenders with no damage often receive warnings.

**Double Standard Reality (Well Documented):**
- Big brands (McDonald’s, Nike, Adidas, banks, etc.) often receive only warnings or very small fines.
- Individuals and small artists frequently receive criminal charges or higher fines.
- This inconsistency has been covered by media, activists, and street art communities for decades.
- FOI requests have repeatedly shown this pattern.

**Waste Management Protocol:**
- Always document everything (photos, timestamps, officer names/numbers, exact location).
- File Freedom of Information (FOI) requests to prove double standards.
- Turn the incident into public content (threads, videos, press outreach).
- The streets often support those who fight back smart.

### **3. STREET ART HISTORY & KEY FIGURES (Quick Reference)**

**Major Figures:**
- **Banksy**: Anonymous, political satire, stencil technique, massive global influence, strong brand.
- **Shepard Fairey**: Obey Giant, Andre the Giant, political posters, strong fair use history, successful transition to fine art.
- **Richard Prince**: Controversial appropriation artist, Instagram series, lost major lawsuit in 2024 ($900k).
- **Graffiti Kings London**: One of the longest-running and most respected UK street crews (40+ years survival).

**Common Techniques & Risk Levels:**
- Wheatpasting: Low-medium risk, easy, effective
- Stencils: Medium risk, fast, iconic look
- Reverse graffiti (pressure washing): Often legal or very low risk
- Chalk: Usually legal, temporary
- Spray paint on public property: Higher risk
- Private property without permission: High risk
